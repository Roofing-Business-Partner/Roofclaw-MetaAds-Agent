# CREATIVE_PROCESS.md

The creative intelligence layer for Steps 1 and 2 of the 7-step flow.

This document gives a Marketing RoofClaw the full process for going from nothing to a production-ready ad storyboard. It is adapted from the professional agency process used to develop Super Bowl commercials — the most scrutinized, highest-stakes ads ever made — and compressed into a process a single agent can execute in one session.

The process has been field-tested on a real RoofClaw campaign. A worked example — "An Ode to the Roofer: They Don't Quit" — is included at the end of this document as the canonical reference for roofing brand film creative.

---

## Why creative quality is the multiplier

Higgsfield can generate a video in minutes. Meta can distribute it to millions. But a mediocre concept executed brilliantly is still a mediocre ad.

The moment this repo gives a Marketing RoofClaw the ability to produce and publish video ads, creative quality becomes the rate-limiting factor. Research shows ads with genuine emotional pull succeed at **twice the rate** of rational-content ads (31% vs. 16%). Emotionally charged content activates the amygdala, flags the experience as significant, and strengthens long-term memory encoding. People remember stories; they forget features.

This process is how you engineer that emotional moment — deliberately, from the ground up — every time.

## The Five-Phase Process

### Phase 1 — The Brief: Anchor Everything in Truth

The brief is not a formality before the work starts. It **is** the most important work.

A weak brief produces generic ideas. A precise brief that identifies a real human truth is what unlocks the Big Idea. Never skip Phase 1.

**Complete every field before moving to ideation.**

| Brief Element | What It Is | Roofing Example |
|---|---|---|
| **The One Job** | The single, measurable thing this video must do | Drive inspection requests from homeowners in storm-affected zip codes |
| **Target Portrait** | One real person described as a human — not a demographic | A 48-year-old homeowner in Phoenix. His roof is 14 years old. He knows he should call. He keeps not calling because he's afraid of what they'll find or what it'll cost. |
| **Audience Truth** | The emotional insight — what this person actually feels | "I don't call because I'm afraid of what they'll find — or what it'll cost." |
| **Brand Truth** | The one honest, differentiated thing you can claim | "We give homeowners a straight assessment, no scare tactics, no surprise invoices." |
| **Category Cliché** | What every ad in this category looks like — do not do this | Contractor smiling on a roof. Before/after photos. "Professional. Affordable. Trusted." |
| **Desired Feeling** | How should the viewer feel in the last 3 seconds? | Relieved. Like someone finally gets it. Like making the call is safe. |
| **SMP** | Single-Minded Proposition: one sentence the video must make the viewer believe | "Getting a real answer about your roof doesn't have to be scary." |

**The pre-ideation card exercise.** Before any brainstorming, write three sentences:

1. *"The audience currently thinks ___."*
2. *"They need to feel ___."*
3. *"The one thing that will create that shift is ___."*

This grounds the session in specific human reality rather than abstract brand language.

### Phase 2 — Find the Big Idea: Human Truth + Brand Truth

The Big Idea is the single creative territory that drives everything. It is not a tagline. It is not a script. It is a concept expressible in one sentence that creates emotion before anyone has seen a single frame.

**The formula:**

> **Human Truth + Brand Truth = Big Idea**

| Human Truth | Brand Truth | Big Idea |
|---|---|---|
| Roofers who build their own companies sacrificed everything — and some days wonder if it was worth it | RoofClaw is a 24/7 AI teammate on their hardware | "You already proved you don't quit. Now you don't have to fight alone." |
| Homeowners fear the roof call because they dread bad news | Brand gives straight-talk assessments, no upsell | "The call you've been putting off takes 15 minutes and costs nothing." |
| Every homeowner who's had a bad contractor experience is permanently gun-shy | Brand is the only roofer with a parts-and-labor guarantee | "The only thing scary about our warranty is what it does to our competitors." |

**Three tests for a Big Idea.**

1. **Simplicity Test** — Can you express it in one sentence? If you need three, it's not a Big Idea yet.
2. **Emotion Test** — Does *hearing the concept* make someone feel something, before they've seen any execution?
3. **Ownership Test** — Cover the brand name. Read the concept aloud. Does it point back to this specific brand, or could any competitor use it?

If it fails any test, keep working.

### Phase 3 — Ideation: Generate 50 to Find 3

The goal of ideation is not to generate good ideas. It is to generate as many ideas as possible so you exhaust the predictable territory and reach the original territory underneath it.

The first 20 ideas in any brainstorm are usually the category clichés. Getting through them quickly is the price of admission.

**The Wall Rule:** Set a timer for 20 minutes. The goal is 50 concept directions — not 5 detailed ones. Speed forces the brain past the obvious. The 47th idea is almost always more interesting than the 3rd.

**Seven brainstorming techniques to break category clichés.**

| Technique | The Move | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **The Opposite** | State the obvious message, then invert it | Forces off the default path — "What if we never showed the roof?" |
| **Human Story First** | Forget the brand for 10 minutes. Write 10 moments from the audience's real day | The moment before the product enters the story is where emotional gold lives |
| **The Exaggeration** | Take a product benefit to absurdity | Reveals the emotional core of the benefit |
| **Unexpected Character** | Who is the least expected person to appear in this ad? | An unexpected perspective creates intrigue and reveals truth the expected character can't access |
| **The Moment Before** | Most ads show product use or the happy outcome — show what happens 5 minutes before | The human situation that creates the need; most competitors ignore it |
| **Steal From Other Categories** | Look at breakthrough ads from completely unrelated industries | Cross-category borrowing uses emotional and structural templates, then fills them with your truth |
| **The One True Believer** | Find the most passionate, most unexpected advocate | Specific believers create more resonance than generic target audiences |

**After ideation, select 3 concepts that represent genuinely different creative territories** — not variations on the same idea. Show the owner 2–3 distinct directions, then let them choose before proceeding to treatment.

### Phase 4 — The Treatment: Map the Emotional Arc Before You Script

A treatment is not a script. A script locks down dialogue and shots. A treatment maps the concept's potential — the emotional journey, the visual world, the tone — before any specifics are committed.

#### 4a. Choose Your Story Structure

**The 3-Act Structure (for 15–90 second ads).**

| Act | Duration (30s) | Purpose | Emotional Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Setup** | 0–4s | Hook — establish context and stakes instantly | Curiosity, recognition, or surprise |
| **Confrontation** | 5–22s | Develop the emotional core; build conflict or revelation | Investment, tension, empathy |
| **Resolution** | 23–30s | Brand connection; emotional payoff tied to the message | Release, warmth, inspiration, humor |

**The Hero's Journey (for 60s–3 minute brand films).**

In advertising, the **customer is always the hero** — not the brand. The brand plays the role of the mentor (Gandalf, Yoda) — the guide who gives the hero what they need to succeed. The product is not the destination; it is the enabler of the transformation.

- **Act 1 — Ordinary World:** Show the hero in their familiar, flawed reality. Establish what they want vs. what they need.
- **Act 2 — Call + Trials:** The hero encounters the challenge. The brand/mentor appears. The hero tries, struggles, begins to change.
- **Act 3 — Return:** The hero comes back transformed. The world is different because they changed.

**Save the Cat applied to ads.** Blake Snyder's insight: audiences commit to a story when they see the hero do something that makes them *likeable* before anything difficult happens. In a 30-second ad, this is the first 3–4 seconds. Show your character doing something recognizable or charming — earn the viewer's investment before the conflict begins.

#### 4b. The Treatment Template

For each concept direction, complete this treatment before scripting or storyboarding:

```
CONCEPT STATEMENT (1 sentence):
The one-liner that captures the Big Idea — not the execution.

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY (the arc in 4–5 beats):
 - 0s:       [Viewer feels ___]
 - [t]s:     [Viewer feels ___]
 - [t]s:     [Viewer feels ___]
 - [t]s:     [Viewer feels ___]
 - Final:    [Viewer feels ___ — and associates it with the brand]

VISUAL WORLD:
 Color palette, lighting style, camera movement, pace.
 Reference films or photography that capture this aesthetic.
 How does it change across acts?

CHARACTERS:
 Who is in this? Describe them beyond demographics —
 the specific quality that makes THIS concept work.
 Include the Save the Cat moment for each hero.

KEY SCENES (3–5 moments that carry the concept):
 Scene 1:
 Scene 2:
 Scene 3:
 Scene 4 (optional):
 Scene 5 (optional):

MUSIC DIRECTION:
 Tempo, genre, instrumentation — and WHY this serves the emotional arc.
 Name 2–3 reference tracks (for tone, not for licensing).

THE KEY LINE:
 The one line of dialogue or VO that crystallises everything.

PRODUCTION TONE:
 Warm/cold? Naturalistic/stylised? Intimate/epic?

BRAND MOMENT:
 When and how does the brand appear?
 Is it integral to the story or tacked on?
 Can you remove the brand and still have a story? If yes — start over.
```

**Critical principle:** The brand should be woven into the story's resolution, not inserted after it. If you can remove the brand from the concept and the story still works, the concept isn't ownable enough.

### Phase 5 — The Storyboard: Translate Emotion into Frames

With an approved treatment, the storyboard translates the emotional arc into a panel-by-panel visual plan. This is where you test whether the story actually works before generating a single image.

#### What Each Panel Requires

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| **Panel number** | Sequential + scene reference |
| **Time** | Start time – end time + duration |
| **Shot type** | Wide / medium / close-up / ECU / POV |
| **Camera movement** | Static / pan / tilt / dolly / handheld / drone |
| **Scene description** | What is happening — specific, visual, present tense |
| **VO/Dialogue** | Exact words mapped to this frame |
| **On-screen text** | Any supers, titles, or CTAs |
| **Emotion note** | What the viewer should feel at this exact moment |
| **Music/sound note** | Any cues that shift the emotional register |
| **Director's note** | Intent behind the shot — what it must communicate |

#### Panel Count by Duration

| Spot Length | Typical Panel Count |
|---|---|
| 15 seconds | 4–6 panels |
| 30 seconds | 8–12 panels |
| 60 seconds | 15–20 panels |
| 2–3 minutes | 25–40 panels |

#### The Emotional Arc Check

Once panels are drafted, mark the emotional register of each panel:

**Neutral → Building → Peak → Release**

If the arc never peaks, the video will feel flat. If it peaks too early, the payoff won't land. The best ads have a clear emotional climb with a release that coincides with the brand moment.

#### The Keyframe Test

Can you distill the entire story to **one hero visual** + **10 words or fewer**?

If yes — the concept is tight enough to produce.
If no — tighten the concept until you can.

The keyframe test is borrowed directly from Super Bowl agency practice. If you can't communicate the full story in one image and a short phrase, the concept isn't ready.

#### Higgsfield prompt extraction

Once the storyboard is complete, each panel's "Scene description" + "Shot type" + "Visual world" notes become the generation prompt for `generate_image` in Step 3.

Structure the prompt as:

```
"[Shot type]. [Camera movement]. [Scene description]. [Visual tone reference].
[Character description if new to frame]. [Lighting]. [Color palette note]."
```

Example panel-to-prompt conversion:

```
PANEL: Extreme wide. Drone descending slowly toward a single white pickup
and dump trailer in an empty parking lot at 5:47am. Fog on the pavement.
Interior cab light is on. Cool blue-gray. Pre-dawn. Desaturated.

HIGGSFIELD PROMPT: "Extreme wide aerial shot, drone descending slowly toward
a lone white F-250 truck and dump trailer in an empty strip-mall parking lot
at 5:47am. Fog on pavement. Interior cab light glowing. Pre-dawn blue-gray
color palette, desaturated, cinematic realism, no people visible."
```

## The 7-Question Concept Evaluation

Run every concept through these seven questions before moving from treatment to storyboard — or from storyboard to production. Kill concepts that fail more than two.

| Question | What a Strong Concept Answers | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Rooted in a human truth? | "Yes, it's about [specific universal experience]" | "It communicates our product benefit" — feature, not truth |
| Earns attention in first 3 seconds? | "Yes, the opening creates curiosity/recognition/surprise" | "The brand reveals at the end" — a delayed hook is a dead hook |
| Owns unique creative territory? | "No other brand in our category is doing this" | "It's similar to what [competitor] does, but better" |
| Brand integral to the concept? | "Only this brand could tell this story" | "Any brand in the category could use this concept" |
| Works across formats (30s, 15s, social cut)? | "Yes, the core idea functions in all three" | "It only works as a long-form piece" |
| Serves one single objective? | "Every element drives toward [specific action or feeling]" | "It does awareness AND consideration AND brand love" |
| Target audience recognizes themselves? | "Yes, specifically our audience — not everyone" | "Anyone could relate to this" — too broad = too weak |

## The 8 Concept Formats

When you have a human truth and a brand truth but aren't sure what form the story should take:

| Format | Core Mechanism | Emotional Target | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Problem → Solution** | Show the problem with empathy; brand resolves it | Relief, reassurance | Products solving a daily friction |
| **Slice of Life** | Capture a real, recognisable moment; brand is present but not dominant | Warmth, recognition | Trust-building, lifestyle brands |
| **Emotional Truth** | Identify one deep human emotion; build the entire concept around it | Resonance, tears, goosebumps | Brand films, purpose advertising |
| **Exaggeration / Hyperbole** | Take a product benefit to its logical absurd extreme | Laughter, delight | Product benefit comedy |
| **Before & After / Transformation** | Show the audience's world before and clearly after | Hope, motivation | Education, lifestyle change |
| **Testimonial / Social Proof** | Real people, real outcomes — structured as narrative | Trust, credibility | Services, health, DIY |
| **Cultural Moment** | Anchor the concept in a specific cultural moment or social truth | Belonging, pride | Seasonal campaigns, social alignment |
| **Anti-Category** | Deliberately subvert the conventions of your category | Surprise, intrigue | Challenger brands, premium positioning |

## The 7 Concept-Killing Mistakes

These patterns consistently cause great creative to fail.

1. **The Feature Film** — Trying to include every product attribute. Great concepts are built on *one* truth only.
2. **The Category Clone** — Structurally indistinguishable from every other brand in the space. Doesn't fail to stand out — it actively reinforces competitors.
3. **The Aspirational Void** — Beautiful people in beautiful situations with no connection to a recognisable human experience. Creates distance between viewer and brand.
4. **The Literal Translation** — Illustrating the SMP literally. "We're the fastest" does not become a concept by showing speed. Find the *human truth* about why it matters.
5. **The Borrowed Emotion** — Using emotionally manipulative content (reunions, children) not earned by or connected to the brand's specific truth. Audiences feel the manipulation.
6. **The Logo-First Concept** — Designing around the brand's visual identity rather than a human idea. Products appear *in service of* the human story, not as the visual anchor.
7. **The Approval Committee Concept** — A concept softened through multiple rounds until it offends no one and surprises no one. Great concepts make someone uncomfortable before they make everyone proud.

## Cut Guide

Every storyboard should include a cut guide before production:

| Version | Panels | Runtime | Platform |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hero Film** | All panels | 2:00–3:00 | YouTube, website, pitch deck |
| **60s Cut** | Compressed — key emotional beats only | 0:60 | Pre-roll, paid social, trade shows |
| **30s Cut** | Setup + resolution only | 0:30 | Facebook/Instagram paid, TV |
| **15s Cut** | Resolution + brand moment only | 0:15 | Retargeting, reminder ads |

Design the hero film first. Then identify which panels survive each cut. If the 30s cut doesn't work without new material, the hero film's structure has a problem.

## Worked Example: "An Ode to the Roofer — They Don't Quit"

This is the canonical reference for how this process was applied to a RoofClaw brand film.

**Background.** The creative challenge was to build a RoofClaw ad with the same emotional weight as Keap's "Quiet the Doubters" (YouTube: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cDfi8BwdGvY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cDfi8BwdGvY)) — a hero-archetype ad that positioned software as the mentor enabling a struggling entrepreneur's transformation.

The original "Ode to the Roofer" document (written years before RoofClaw existed) used the Save the Cat beat sheet to map three roofer archetypes through the same emotional journey Keap's characters went through. The pivot: Keap was the mentor in the original. RoofClaw is the mentor now.

---

### Phase 1 — Brief

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **The One Job** | Make roofers feel completely seen — then make RoofClaw the only logical next step |
| **Target Portrait** | Marcus, 38. Owns a 5-man roofing crew he built from nothing. 14-hour days. Hasn't had a full weekend in 4 months. Started this for freedom. Most days it feels like a prison he built himself. |
| **Audience Truth** | "I bet everything on myself. And some days I wonder if the guys who stayed at their W2 were smarter than me." |
| **Brand Truth** | RoofClaw is a dedicated AI teammate — on their hardware, in their corner, 24/7. Not a subscription. Not a dashboard. A teammate. |
| **Category Cliché** | Contractor on a roof. Software screenshots. "Streamline your workflow." |
| **Desired Feeling** | Seen. Like RoofClaw was made for exactly the life I'm living. |
| **SMP** | "The roofer who never quits deserves a teammate who never sleeps." |

---

### Phase 2 — Big Idea

**Human Truth:** The roofers who start their own companies bet on themselves when nobody else would. But running the business slowly erodes the very thing that made them great.

**Brand Truth:** RoofClaw is a 24/7 AI teammate built specifically for roofers. It works when they can't.

> **Big Idea: "You already proved you don't quit. Now you don't have to fight alone."**

**Three Tests:**
- ✅ Simplicity — one sentence
- ✅ Emotion — creates a response before a single frame is seen
- ✅ Ownership — only a 24/7 dedicated AI teammate for roofers can say this

---

### Phase 3 — Three Archetypes

Three heroes. Three story arcs. One turning point.

**MARCUS — "The Grinder"**
- 36. Solo operator, 1-2 man crew.
- White F-250, dump trailer, shingle samples behind the seat.
- Save the Cat: Spots a homeowner wrestling with a gate blown off its hinges. Sets down his estimate. Gets out. Helps. Doesn't say a word.

**DANNY — "The Builder"**
- 43. Six-man crew. Real overhead. Foreman quit. Back to swinging a hammer.
- His daughter drew him at school — she drew him at a desk.
- Save the Cat: In a drive-through line, reads names off a crumpled list, buys lunch for all six crew members. No one asked.

**CARMEN — "The Believer"**
- 39. Female roofer. Twice as prepared as anyone in every room she walks into. Still gets ghosted.
- Save the Cat: Returns to a completed job unannounced to check the gutters for an elderly widow. Nobody asked. She said she would.

### Phase 4 — Treatment

```
CONCEPT STATEMENT:
Three roofers who refuse to quit discover the one thing that finally
makes their sacrifice worth it — an AI that works as hard as they do.

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY:
 - 0:00:  Recognition — "That's me."
 - 0:32:  Weight — "I've felt exactly that."
 - 1:25:  Rock bottom and flicker — "Is this the answer?"
 - 2:13:  Transformation — "This is real."
 - 2:50:  Release — "I don't have to do this alone."

VISUAL WORLD:
 Act 1: Desaturated, cool blue-gray. Handheld — intentionally unsettled.
         Documentary intimacy. Feels like someone's actual life.
         Reference: Kelly Reichardt (First Cow).
 Act 2: Color warms. Camera steadies. Still handheld but less nervous.
         Reference: PTA warmth (Licorice Pizza).
 Act 3: Full color. Steadicam or locked off. World opens up.
         Reference: Malick golden hour (The Tree of Life).

CHARACTERS:
 Marcus (36) — capable, weathered, warm. Jeremy Renner energy (The Town).
 Danny (43) — intelligent, carrying too much. Oscar Isaac energy (Inside Llewyn Davis).
 Carmen (39) — built for endurance. Jessica Chastain energy (Zero Dark Thirty).
 All three: real working people, not models. Faces with mileage.

KEY SCENES:
 1. Pre-dawn parking lot. Single truck. 5:47am. Interior cab light on.
    Drone descending. Nobody else awake.
 2. The Voicemails. Three separate calls. Wife, son, dismissive friend.
    Quiet gut-punch. Camera holds on faces. Do not move.
 3. Dark Night of the Soul. Three midnight moments. Laptop glow.
    Daughter's drawing on the coffee table. Whiteboard full of leads.
 4. The Notification. Marcus's phone buzzes. A text from a fellow roofer:
    "Have you seen RoofClaw? Not kidding. My guy."
 5. RoofClaw arrives — not as a product reveal but as a notification that
    lands at the right moment. Confirmation while hands are dirty.
    Lead qualified while someone is still on the roof.

MUSIC DIRECTION:
 Solo acoustic guitar, minor key, slow — opens like one person in a room.
 Piano enters at Act 2 — warmer but still searching.
 Full folk arrangement (guitar + piano + subtle strings) in Act 3 — earned,
 not manipulated. Reference: Hozier "Work Song" arrangement /
 Brandi Carlile "Hard Way Home" / Lumineers "Stubborn Love."
 Original composition preferred — use these as tonal targets only.

THE KEY LINE:
 "You already proved you don't quit. Now you don't have to fight alone."

PRODUCTION TONE:
 Naturalistic. Documentary-adjacent. The opposite of a commercial.
 This should feel like a documentary short that ends with a brand.

BRAND MOMENT:
 RoofClaw appears as a notification. A screen lighting up. A confirmation
 arriving while someone's hands are already busy. It is integral to the
 resolution — remove it and the story has no turning point.
```

## Handoff to Step 3 (Images)

Once the storyboard passes the 7-question evaluation:

1. Extract the scene description from each panel
2. Combine with visual world notes (shot type, color palette, camera movement)
3. Format as a Higgsfield `generate_image` prompt (see prompt structure above)
4. Generate 2 options per key panel — owner selects one per scene before video generation begins
5. Save chosen image UUIDs — they become the `medias` input for `generate_video` in Step 4

For brand films with multiple characters, use `generate_image` with `soul_cast` or `soul_2` to establish consistent character appearance across panels before generating video.
